Salta Province

It boasts some of the most spectacular scenery in the country, with vast mountains and valleys, and has a rich cultural heritage from Incan and other indigenous cultures. To inspire you, here are eight reasons to visit Salta.

While Argentina is fairly averse to spices of any heat, the northern Andean region has a food culture that is very different to the rest of the country. Salta boasts arguably some of the best food in the country.

You cannot leave Salta without trying empanadas salteñas (the best empanadas in the country), locro (a hearty corn and meat stew), tamales, and humitas (a corn mixture stuffed with meat).

One thing to keep in mind when travelling to Salta is that the distances between places are large, and the roads are not always in the best condition.

Salta City

Nicknamed "Salta, La Linda", Salta boasts impeccably preserved colonial architecture and two of the most striking churches in Argentina: the pretty pink Cathedral of Salta and the red and gold Iglesia San Francisco.

The Train to the Clouds

This breathtaking railway route is the 5th highest in the world! It runs to the Viaducto de La Polvorilla and passes through the copper mining town of San Antonio de Los Cobres along a truly spectacular route.

Quebrada de Humahuaca

A deep valley carved out by the Rio Grande, the Quebrada de Humahuaca is an area rich in ancient Incan history and culture. Exploring the colonial streets and architecture of Humahuaca, as well as the surrounding area, is an amazing adventure. One of the best drives in the world and there is a cave cathedral where musicians play.

The most distinctive feature of Salta’s other-worldly landscapes is the multi-hued mountains, with their soft folds.

The most famous is the Hill of Seven Colors, in Quebrada de Humahuaca. While this attraction is technically in neighbouring Jujuy, it is most commonly reached on a day trip from Salta.

Salinas Grandes

Ringed by mountains, this area of snow-white salt flats is a good place to spot llamas and vicuñas

Cafayete

Salta boasts an incredible wine region of its own that shouldn’t be missed. The laid-back small town is surrounded by lush green vineyards, a kind of oasis in the midst of Salta’s desert-like environment. Oh, and the wine is fantastic too! Grown at high altitudes, Salta’s signature varietal is Torrontés, a refreshing white wine.

Argentina’s second center for quality wine production, Cafayate is a popular tourist destination but still has a tranquil small-town feel. It’s spectacularly scenic, with the green of the vines backed by soaring mountains beyond, and is one of northwest Argentina’s most seductive destinations.

Mendoza Province

Argentina’s main wine region at the foot of the Andes.

A bustling city of wide, leafy avenues, atmospheric plazas and cosmopolitan cafes, Mendoza is a trap. Even if you’ve (foolishly) only given it a day or two on your itinerary, you’re bound to end up hanging around, captivated by the laid-back pace, while surrounded by every possible comfort.

All over the country (and in much of the world), the name Mendoza is synonymous with wine, and this is the place to base yourself if you’re up for touring the vineyards, taking a few dozen bottles home or just looking for a good vintage to accompany the evening’s pizza.

La Payunia

A remote land of rosy lava, ebony gorges, deep karstic caves and flamingo-flecked lagoons

Mendoza

The Reserva Provincial La Payunia also known as Payún or Payén is a natural reserve located in the Malargüe Department in the southern part of Mendoza Province, Argentina

Termas de Cacheuta

Overlooking the Mendoza River, just outside the city is the Termas de Cacheuta hot springs. The spa provides a circuit of pools ranging in temperatures winding through indoors and outdoors, as well as other treats like a water volcano and bubble baths to enjoy along the way. There is also natural steam room and other spa treatments included.

Bariloche (Lake District)

Swiss-themed town has improbable wooden chalets, chocolate shops and even St Bernard dogs to give it an Alpine feel. Nahuel Huapi park nearby, Argentina’s Lake District has pristine alpine-like scenery, dramatic mountain lakes and ancient trees.
